Lately, I keep thinking about the phrase "life is a practice."

I used to think it was a cliché — the kind of thing you'd find printed on a mug.

But after going through some things these past two years, I've started to believe it's actually true — it's just that most people misunderstand what it means.

## Practice Isn't About Suffering

It's not about pulling all-nighters, going on extreme diets, pushing your limits, and then posting about it on social media.

The way I understand it, "practice" means **correction**.

Correcting the way you see things. Correcting your knee-jerk reactions. Correcting the thought patterns that keep dragging you into the same pit.

It's not about becoming more capable — it's about running a little smoother.

## So What Are We Correcting?

After thinking it over, I'd say roughly three things.

**Cognition.**

Most of the time, what hurts me isn't the thing itself — it's my interpretation of it.

The same event, seen from a different angle, can lead to a completely different conclusion. This ability to "shift perspective" isn't innate — it has to be trained.

**Emotions.**

I used to either suppress them entirely or let them sweep me away.

Over time, I've come to realize that emotions are actually signals — they're telling you something hasn't been dealt with yet.

Learning to listen to them instead of fighting them has been far harder than I imagined.

**Relationships.**

With others: less urge to control, more genuine curiosity.

With yourself: less internal friction, and making sure your words and actions actually align.

## The Dojo Is Life Itself

You can't wait until you're "ready" to start practicing.

It's not in some temple. It's not on the last page of some book.

All the real lessons are hidden in daily life —

The moment you're misunderstood, you're practicing emotional boundaries.

The moment you hesitate before temptation, you're practicing desire and choice.

The moment you're lost after failure, you're practicing cognition and resilience.

**You don't finish practicing and then go live your life. Life keeps forcing you to practice.**

You think you're just getting through the day, but you've been in class the whole time. The only question is whether you've noticed.

## Life Is Not a Game

A lot of people like to say "life is a game." I get the appeal.

It makes things feel lighter. It makes losing less scary. It makes you feel like everything is still salvageable.

But I can't truly buy into it.

Games are meant to be won. If you fail, you restart. Don't like this save file? Load another one. The logic feels great, but it hides an escape hatch: since it's just a game, losing doesn't count, pain doesn't matter much, and there's always a next round.

The problem is, in real life, many things don't have a next round.

Some relationships, once broken, stay broken. Some words, once spoken, can't be taken back. Some people walk away, and you never get a chance to choose differently.

Facing these moments with a gamer's mindset makes it easy to slip into a gentle kind of avoidance — it looks carefree, but it's really just not daring to face things honestly.

**The logic of practice is entirely different.**

There's no concept of "beating the game," and there are no winners. It only asks you one thing: **This time, did you see a little more clearly than last time?**

Failure isn't "losing health points" — it's real feedback.

Pain isn't punishment — it's a signal that something hasn't been dealt with.

It asks you to catch every single thing — no skipping, no fast-forwarding, no pretending it didn't happen.

A gamer's mindset keeps you light, but it's easy to float away. A practitioner's mindset keeps you grounded, but it's easy to sink.

**I don't want to live this life as if it were a game.**

Not because there's anything wrong with it, but because I'm afraid that in the comfort of "it doesn't count anyway," I'll end up treating the things that truly matter as just another level I can skip.
